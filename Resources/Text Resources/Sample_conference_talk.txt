WHAT IS DATA GOVERNANCE?

• Data Governance is a collection of  practices and

processes that help to ensure the formal management of

data assets within an organization

• It’s part of  a broader change management competency

• But it is also a competency or center-of-excellence

function to guide independent actors

• Data Governance helps our organizations do data better

WHAT DATA GOVERNANCE IS REALLY ALL
ABOUT

• There is value buried in our data, and if  left to chaos

we won’t realize much of  that value

• We need to be deliberate with our data so that it is
reliable for use and organized in an intuitive way

• People need coordinating to provide the scale and

consistency necessary for data to drive value at scale

• The value of  Data Governance is only exposed

through the use of  the data it governs


--- PAGE BREAK ---

BUSINESS CHALLENGES ADDRESSED BY DATA
GOVERNANCE

• Codifying data standards, policies, and procedures

• Creating data definitions

• Assigning data owners and data stewards

• Establishing a Data Governance council to make decisions

• Resolving conflicts among data stakeholders

• Delegating responsibility for data decision-making

• Coordinating with a project management office (PMO)

•

Influencing technology systems development

• Aligning data assets and activities to organizational goals (data strategy)

• Building a quantitative culture through training and other communications

WHAT DATA GOVERNANCE DOES NOT DO

• Data Governance by itself  does not accomplish much at all

• It is through Data Governance’s impact on other functions

where it becomes valuable

• Data Governance guides but does not directly build

• It does not guarantee success in business, or even with data

• Data Governance is not magic

WHY DATA GOVERNANCE IS IMPORTANT

• Data is used everywhere in our organizations

• We often lack coordination to ensure consistent and

reliable use of the same data

• Relying on people doing “the best they can” is the default

in many organizations

• This results in data of unknown and varying quality

• In turn, this data then drives business processes and results

in unpredictable ways

• Leading to deficiencies in business results = less successful

organizations

HOW DATA GOVERNANCE HAS EVOLVED

Earlier Days

Today

• Data systems used to be highly centralized,
driven by detailed specifications, heavy and
difficult to change

• Data Governance efforts looked similar:
centralized authoritative bodies radiating
policy and standards

• Data systems are now largely decentralized,
nimble, and adapting quickly using new
technologies to seize new business
opportunities

• Data Governance needs to be similarly
responsive, providing coordination in
countless scenarios without bottlenecking

progress


WHY “CHECKING THE BOXES” MAY GET YOU STARTED,
AND HOW TO MOVE BEYOND THIS
• Many Data Governance coordination efforts are started in response to
regulatory mandates, audit findings, or some other compliance-related
stipulation

• This is OK – we are happy any time and any way Data Governance gets

going

• But if we do not recognize the full breadth of the Data Governance

potential, we will not only miss out on the bigger picture – but we will
likely fail at the original goal of checking boxes!

• This is because Data Governance done to satisfy an external constraint

inevitably becomes an endless resource drain

• If we can pivot Data Governance to being a net-positive return on

resources, then we can build momentum

DEFINING AND SELLING THE DATA GOVERNANCE
MISSION

• The ironic thing is that Data Governance needs to be everything to

everybody, but we cannot start there

• Value is determined by measuring the outputs from data-related use cases

• This means that our Data Governance efforts must align to support

valuable use cases

• This is how we can generate organizational (macro) support for Data

Governance

• But generating support at the individual (micro) level is a different challenge

• We will ultimately have to align incentives, motivate, and solve for “why”

before anything we talk about from a “how” perspective becomes
sustainable

WHAT SEPARATES IMPACTFUL DATA
GOVERNANCE
• Real value comes only from impacting business outcomes

• These can be measured three ways:

• Revenues
• Costs
• Risks

• Data Governance tends to be several abstractions removed from

actual value-creating activities

• This does not absolve DG from being held accountable to value

• Strong Data Governance embraces this and builds the ability to

maximize its value contribution


--- PAGE BREAK ---

WHY MANY DATA GOVERNANCE EFFORTS
STRUGGLE

• Data Governance activities are often multiple steps removed

from real value

• Failing to quantify the impact of Data Governance efforts

• A tendency to focus inward on what is being done instead of

outward on the impact being made

• Going down paths of little relevancy to the business because of

well-intended but flawed reasoning

• A lack of resource commitment

• Poorly aligned incentives to get people to participate

• A lack of authority or accountability

AVOIDING IMPOSSIBLE SITUATIONS

• Data Governance is tough to get right, even in ideal circumstances

• But the reality is often far from ideal, and DG is often viewed as a “nice-to-

have” – so resource commitments can waver

• The techniques we’ve discussed today are the best defense to unreasonable

expectations

• By promoting transparency, prioritization, and tradeoffs, we can usually

find a reasonable path forward

• When forced to betray these principles, we have a much tougher path

ahead – continue to focus on transparency, communication, collaboration
to resolve challenges

• Measuring and documentation can be powerful allies


--- PAGE BREAK ---

THE ART OF POSITIONING FOR THE LONG AND
SHORT TERMS
• Data Governance is a long game won in the short term

• Long-term accountability only exists through the aggregation

of short-term results

• Efforts can often get away with a lack of accountability or

results in the short term

• Do not fall into the trap!

• We cannot go back in time to repair our past credibility

• From the very beginning, we must hold ourselves accountable

UNDERSTANDING THE ROLES AND DISTINGUISHING
BETWEEN DATA STEWARDS, DATA CUSTODIANS, AND
OTHER STAKEHOLDERS
• Some Data Governance terminology seems to be universally

confusing

• Data stewards: are tasked with activities that have direct and

material impact on the value of  data

• Data custodians: often like data stewards but tend to be lower-level
and more often technical, and with less clear impacts on the data.
Are tasked less with changing data for the better, and more with
trying not to make it worse

• But the terms themselves can be whatever you make of  them!


--- PAGE BREAK ---

WHAT IT MEANS TO BE A “DATA OWNER” – AND
WHY SOME PEOPLE MAY NOT WANT TO BE ONE

• This one gets its own slide because it is both important and highly

charged

• Data owners are often the supreme subject matter experts for a domain

of  data

• These folks tend to be very busy and cannot possibly add one more thing
to their overtaxed day, but will often agree and then not put in the time

• Some will assign proxies, which can work, but only if  they are delegated

actual decision-making authority as well

• To some people, data ownership sounds an awful lot like a future bus to

be thrown under

• You don’t become somebody that gets asked to be a data owner without

some amount of  political savvy

ESTABLISHING A MODEL TO FIT DATA GOVERNANCE
FUNCTIONS ALONGSIDE EXISTING WORK

• We have talked a lot about structure so far, but now process is necessary

• Rule #1: Do not start by having meetings to figure out a good process

• Meetings are good for critiquing the process and adding additional

ideas, but they are no way to fill a blank sheet of paper

• Recognize that most (or all) of the people involved with Data

Governance will have other responsibilities that will likely take
precedence

• This is a challenge, not a problem. The last thing we’d want is Data

Governance being done by a bunch of people who have no idea how
the business actually works


--- PAGE BREAK ---

THINKING ABOUT THE OBVIOUS QUESTION

• If the previous slide has you asking, “How can we get people to

do DG work?” then you are thinking about this correctly

• This is the hardest part of Data Governance

• Policies and rules, charters and role definitions, these are simple

by comparison

• In fact, many people think of those as Data Governance
activities – they are actually pre-Data Governance when
considered more deeply

• We must apply them successfully to govern data, and this starts

with motivating people to do DG work

ESTIMATING AND COMMUNICATING THE
EXPECTED BENEFITS

• We will cover a lot about motivating folks to get involved, but first we need to

understand why this all matters

• We need to quantify the value of Data Governance as much as we can

• This will start as estimates, taking baseline measurements, and looking at

external research for benchmarks

• Use these to establish success criteria that are as objective as possible and can be

agreed to by the many Data Governance stakeholders

• It is fine to debate estimates and benchmarks, and even measurements in some

cases, but work to establish agreement on the evaluation process early on

• This will make generating lasting momentum easier, because when the initial
excitement and interest wane (and they will), we will need good data to justify
Data Governance (in more ways than one!)

GETTING STARTED WITH DATA GOVERNANCE

Data Governance In Motion

LEARN WHY STARTING WITH “WHO CAN HELP
US?” IS A RECIPE FOR DISASTER

• We’ve talked a lot about structure, planning, and driving interest

• But thinking about Data Governance as “something important that

people should help make happen” is backwards logic

• Data Governance should be considered “a set of tools and

capabilities that can help people do important things with data”

• To make successful Data Governance, we cannot focus on the

success of Data Governance

• We must instead focus on the success we help others achieve

• This manifests in servant leadership, where we use whatever we

have to help as best we can, and ask for help as a means to an end


--- PAGE BREAK ---

LEARN WHY STARTING WITH MEETINGS IS ALSO
A RECIPE FOR DISASTER

• This is a potential issue when DG structure is given too much thought, too early

•

If  we get a Data Governance group of  folks together and say, “Go meet and do
data-governancy stuff,” then they will get nothing done

• Think about efficiency – writing a sentence is easier for one individual than for

three, or 12

• Fit the form to the function

•

If  you don’t know which DG activities need to get done, who will? Can you
identify anybody better?

• The best approach is to push a bit further in doing it yourself  than you

reasonably should, and then have a meeting to present your thoughts and solicit
questions and feedback

HOW TO DOCUMENT AND ORGANIZE THE
ARTIFACTS OF DATA GOVERNANCE

• First, do not think this is as important as building the artifacts themselves

• But a frightening number of organizations mess this up

• Make Data Governance artifacts as easy as possible to find and use!

• Do not lock them away in a file “share” with limited access

• Intranet home page links are good

• Data catalogs are good, great if widely used

• Posters around the office are cheesy, but also good

• Data Governance artifacts are our product, and products need customers!


--- PAGE BREAK ---

HOW COMMS AND MARKETING CAN MAKE THE
DIFFERENCE BETWEEN FAILURE AND SUCCESS

• We should think of  Data Governance as an internal business

• People don’t buy things they don’t know about – see the previous slide

• The best thing is that most people don’t need to buy Data Governance artifacts with dollars, they

pay simply by using them

• This should be the most successful business in the world with economics like that!

• Yet we often fail to generate enough interest in something that costs our customers no dollars and

exists for the sole purpose of  helping people improve the value they get from data

•

It’s because they don’t know it exists, or how to use the artifacts, or how to change their behavior in
response to data insights, or that it is Data Governance that makes “data-driven” possible

• This is not just why marketing and communications are important to Data Governance – these

ARE Data Governance

TECHNIQUES FOR CONDUCTING EFFECTIVE
MEETINGS AND WORKSHOPS

• Always have an agenda, and distribute it with the meeting request

• Assign a secretary who is not leading the meeting to take notes and

compile/distribute minutes

•

If  the meeting is taking place online, consider recording it for those who cannot
attend

• Ask all participants to take notes and send them to the secretary

• Engage all attendees directly at least once in the session (this is surprisingly

effective)

• Encourage attendees not needed to conduct the business of  the meeting to

instead review minutes or watch the recording, but encourage participation from
anyone who wants to be part of  it

• Keep the meeting moving with a stated goal of  ending early

GETTING READY TO LAUNCH

• It’s best to form as much of a plan as possible with a small team before

trying to generate broader interest

• People respond better when they see details – even if they disagree with

them, this is a positive step

• These getting-ready-to-start efforts should split between the primary

challenge of establishing Data Governance and the secondary challenge
of having it matter to the business

• Establishing DG is relatively easy, but necessary – making a difference is

much more difficult, and also necessary

• Early actions (not planning activities) will set the tone and can make or

break the overall effort

YOUR ACTION PLAN FOR THE FIRST THREE
MONTHS

• “Low-hanging fruit” is a bad term – focus not on what is easy to do, but what

can be most immediately valuable in the short term

• This allows us to operationalize DG in a real way while also satisfying the

marketing component that is just as important

• Some possible DG activities:

• Defining commonly used data and acronyms
• Articulating a regulatory compliance or privacy policy for data and evaluating current

issues

• Establishing a suitability-for-use scoring methodology for data quality
• Compiling business and technical metadata to support an ongoing initiative that is

struggling

• Data lineage analysis for key reports


--- PAGE BREAK ---

SCALING INITIAL PLANS INTO LONGER TIME
FRAMES

• The collection of initial activities should naturally build towards something

more substantial

• Longer-term planning can’t simply be tactical – it must align to business

and data strategy

• The longer the view, the higher the grain, and the more likely it will change

• But momentum needs to build, so the short-term objectives should be

considered as Part 1’s of multipart threads

• This is an exercise in storytelling, also – long-term plans are about vision

and dreams

• The long term only arrives in short terms, so a regular update and

communication cadence is essential

IDEAS FOR GENERATING INTEREST AMONG PEOPLE
WHO WOULD RATHER NOT BE BOTHERED BY THIS

• Even with the best roadmap, plans, communications, support, etc., we will still

find plenty of folks that just won’t care about Data Governance

• They tend to have just as many data problems as everybody else

• They may be intimidated by the technology components, or feel overwhelmed

by their existing responsibilities and are shut off to anything else

• It’s not usually as personal as it often feels

• We cannot win over everybody, but we can serve everybody – if we orient our
energies towards how we can help our detractors, we may find more willing
participants

• Always try to solve for “WIIFM?” (What’s in It for Me?)


--- PAGE BREAK ---

SIMPLE ENCOURAGEMENTS AND INCENTIVES

• Awareness is perhaps the easiest and most cost-effective

encouragement to drive interest

• A table outside the cafeteria during “Data Governance Week” can

be surprisingly effective, especially alongside giving away cookies or
some other happiness

• Public recognition can work well to encourage more active
participation, and works even better in larger organizations

• Team goals and challenges with a nominal award (happy hour, pizza
party, personal day, trophy) can be great to get more collective results
across a team

• Transparency through publishing metrics by area, calling out both
positive and negative outliers, can be a stronger tactic with lower
costs (but a certain amount of political risk)

WHY IT IS SO IMPORTANT TO GET PARTICIPATION
FROM THE PEOPLE WHO STAND TO GAIN THE MOST
• Remember “WIIFM?” – this motivates people perhaps better than

anything

• The people that stand to benefit most will be the most likely to be

interested, so even if they are not ideal for doing the work, get them
involved

• Or see if you can complete a cycle where A helps B who helps C who

helps A

• The goal is to create perpetual motion through self-reinforcing behavior

patterns

• This is key to building lasting momentum that will grow without

needing outside resource investments

• The previous slide covered sparks, but we need fuel to keep the fire

going

ABSTRACT

Data Governance is the combination of two familiar words which, when combined, can confuse organizations of all
sizes. These words represent a valuable ideal which is exceedingly difficult to achieve under the best of circumstances.
When just starting out, it can be completely overwhelming.

Fear not, as many others have been down this road before! Fortunately for you, our collective experience has unveiled
many lessons about the journey to successful Data Governance. Interestingly, some early mistakes appear completely
logical, yet cause immeasurable difficulties in establishing long-term success.

This tutorial breaks down the whole mess and provides real help in how to get started with Data Governance. We
will give you tools to teach your organization what successful Data Governance is all about and give you techniques
to start making an impact right away. Highlights Include:

• Learn the fundamental principles and functions of Data Governance

• Learn how to structure an effective and sustainable Data Governance organization

• Explore the concept of frameworks and how to apply them

• How to generate interest and support for Data Governance

• Get tips to avoid common pitfalls

DISTILLING CHAOS INTO CLARITY

• As information is collected, it is necessary to put it into a

form that can be acted upon

• This will require simplification and deduplication

• A spreadsheet is an excellent tool for this

• But try to not over-document and make it too dense

• Use more jumps if  needed to keep things simple

• We’re aiming for something comprehensive that will be

quantified and then used for DG initiatives prioritization

APPLYING A FRAMEWORK FOR ANALYSIS

• Once a collection of findings is available, we can start analyzing

• We do not need to have information gathering completed to start

• In fact, it is impractical to wait for everything before getting

started on downstream tasks

• It is OK to leverage multiple frameworks to drive different

viewpoints of analysis

• But the more complexity we introduce in analysis, the more

difficulty we will have in communicating the results


--- PAGE BREAK ---

RECONCILING BUSINESS WANTS AND NEEDS
WITH REALITY
• Categorize the findings using your framework(s) of choice

• Taxonomical categorization is usually overkill and/or impossible –

think tagging more than hierarchies

• This will depend somewhat on the framework(s) you use

• Focus energies at this point on capturing objective truths

• Variables and estimates can be debated, which we will encourage

later

• But it is much easier to do so alongside a sound categorization

process

• At this stage we will begin to identify opportunities to take action

• Note them objectively and start capturing cost estimates, but don’t

focus on planning or prioritizing anything yet

DATA GOVERNANCE IS NOT JUST ABOUT DATA

• Data Governance does not do much without change

• This must be aligned with existing project and program

• Data Governance-driven change will cut across those initiatives

just like data itself is used in multiple ways

• Because Data Governance artifacts tend towards the intangible,

PRIORITIZING OBJECTIVES AND IDENTIFYING

• Once we have done isolated analysis of problems,

towards prioritization

• Priorities must take into account benefits and costs

• The temptation of outcome-only prioritization leads

to impossible situations

• Our focus should be on presenting clear options and

tradeoffs to DG Steering

• The business should ultimately set priorities – either

--- PAGE BREAK ---

BASIC APPROACH TO QUANTIFIED DATA
GOVERNANCE
• There is a continuum of types of measures:

• Inputs: activity, direct control

• Outputs: results, direct influence

• Impacts: value, indirect influence

• Inputs are easiest to measure, but least important overall

• Impacts are the most important, but can be extremely

difficult to quantify

• Outputs are in the middle, but run the risk of being used

as a proxy for value – they are not the same thing!

KEY TERMS FOR QUANTIFICATION

• Measurement – a data point that changes over time

• Metric – a collection or calculation using measures for comparative

purposes

• KPI – special metrics that we exert influence over, used to track

performance relative to an established target. Often uses a red-yellow-
green performance indicator

• Baseline – initial measurements or metrics from before a change is

made

• Benchmark – a level of expected results determined from similar

situations, typically from different parties

• Target – a goal state to be achieved through upcoming activities


--- PAGE BREAK ---

LEARN USEFUL MEASUREMENTS, METRICS, AND
KPIS FOR DATA GOVERNANCE

• Inputs

• Terms defined
• Data stewards trained
•
Issue resolution time

• Outputs

• Data catalog usage
• Data-driven behaviors
• Net promoter scores

• Impacts

• Operational efficiency improvements (cost)
• Sales improvements (revenue)
• Regulatory certifications (risk)

TECHNIQUES FOR BENCHMARKING AND
MONITORING PROGRESS

• First, start measuring whatever you can, even if you have no idea what

you are doing or what you are going to use it for later

• Don’t overdo laborious measures, but at least capture the easy stuff

• Benchmarks are most often drawn from external sources and research

• In setting targets, revise to fit appropriately with your organization

• Look to set up automated measures and reporting results wherever

possible

• The more friction the quantification process has, the more difficult it

will be to keep it going


--- PAGE BREAK ---

DATA DEBT – AND HOW TO ADDRESS IT

• Data debt is a sibling of technical debt, and represents data work that

needs to be completed at some point, but is skipped in the short term to
satisfy another, typically more urgent, objective

• The idea is if we accumulate data debt without ever taking responsibility

and addressing it, we will put ourselves into data bankruptcy

• There is a bit of theoretical slant to this, but the principles are valid, and
measures will help us more transparently evaluate and treat data debt

• We should keep this in mind as we quantify Data Governance, because

DG is how we coordinate the activities that pay down data debt

• Data debt may be a good way to help demonstrate the value of DG efforts,
especially in situations where impact measurements are difficult to develop

IDENTIFYING WHEN SOMETHING IS NOT
WORKING

• Measures need to be watched and reviewed regularly for them

to be worthwhile

• KPIs are especially helpful to drive action, as long as they are

not ignored

• It is fine, necessary even, to update targets or swap in new

measures as business circumstances evolve

• Be cautious not to massage targets or select data to fit a

preconceived story line

• Better to adjust activities to result in better measures – this is

the whole point!

• Proactively identifying issues and then coming up with proposed solutions

is a good way to disarm these scenarios

• People get tired of being presented problems alone, so if we can present a
balanced problem and solution it will be easier for folks to approve and
save their mental energy

• Data Governance needs to be action-oriented as much as possible, but

there is a place for analysis and more contemplative strategy and planning

--- PAGE BREAK ---

THE MANY LAYERS OF TRANSPARENCY

• We talk a lot about transparency in general, but it is worth spending a

moment on specifics

• There are several components to transparency:

• Availability – are the measures easy to find and access?
• Accuracy – is the underlying data suitable to draw the conclusions being

made?

• Objectivity – do the components and the overall conclusions reflect truth?
• Clarity – are the findings easy to understand?
• Actionable – do the insights lead to productive actions or decisions?

• These might extend beyond a traditional view of transparency, but if we
were to remove any of them, would things move more or less towards
perfect transparency?

UNDERSTANDING GRANULARITY

• Granularity (or grain) is a synonym for precision

• It’s all about how much detail is included in what we are doing

with data

• The lower-level the granularity of something is, the more detailed it

is. This is in comparison to high-level

• Whereas higher precision means more detailed. This can get

confusing

• Data always has a level of native granularity that may or may not

be possible to change

• Granularity is important at every part of the data lifecycle, but

perhaps most important as we look to share information

• The level of granularity we need correlates to the types of decisions

needing to be made


--- PAGE BREAK ---

WHY LESS IS USUALLY MORE, ESPECIALLY FOR
EXECUTIVES

• Executives make high-level decisions with major consequences, often driven by

incomplete information

• To wait for complete information may often mean losing out on a potential

opportunity

• Unnecessary details can make executive-level decision-making more difficult

• People are biologically wired to conserve mental energy, and find it far easier to

make decisions when the amount of information is in alignment with the
magnitude of the decision

• We see this in our daily lives when ordering off a restaurant menu – is it easier or
more difficult to make a decision when the menu is enormous, with every option
imaginable?

• We don’t always need to include every bit of supporting information to drive the

right insights and resulting actions

VISUALIZATION TOOLS

• Visualizations are especially helpful for people to consume information to

drive decisions and activities

• They allow us to see numbers over time and react to trends

• Business intelligence dashboards often allow us to interact directly and drill

down into more details when things look “interesting”

• The danger with visualizations is that they are prone to oversimplify and
may unintentionally (or intentionally) mislead data consumers through
questionable designs

• Pretty visualizations can also obfuscate Data Quality, especially where Data

Quality scoring is unavailable

• Also, some visualizations are inherently worthless, despite looking visually

interesting

HOW TO DETERMINE TITLES AND ROLES

• Data Governance titles are a tool for communication

• DG participants can better understand their roles and expectations

• Other DG stakeholders have a better gauge on what is happening

• Beyond this, titles are flexible, and should be adapted to the needs and culture

of your organization

• Common titles: data steward, DG coordinator, data custodian, data or
domain owner, subject matter expert (SME), executive sponsor, DG
committee member, metadata manager, technical SME, business process
owner, data quality manager, master data manager, data catalog
administrator

• DG titles and roles can overlap with a “regular” job, or can be somebody’s
full-time role the more DG activities are operationalized into the regular
workings of the business

WHAT IT MEANS TO BE A DATA OWNER

• Data ownership is a particularly charged title worth spending a little time

discussing

• Data owners tend to be identified as the most knowledgeable person
about a data domain or area of  the business reflected in the data

• These folks are often not directly involved in the Data Governance stand-
up effort until they are asked to join it – they are usually already busy
doing work in their area of  the business

• The data ownership assignment conversation can be uncomfortable – this

can feel like a bull’s-eye more than a beneficial role to the individual

• Time commitments can also be an issue – the most effective data owners
delegate almost all of  their work to others, and then review or resolve
escalations as appropriate

•

It’s almost like data owners are in charge of  their own little country or
fiefdom, and then come together with others to coordinate on issues that
impact across sovereign lines

OTHER DG TITLES YOU MAY NEED

• “Data Steward” is a generic term that can be useful but can also mean nothing

• “Data Custodian” is a term that actually has a great literal meaning but in reality
should not often be used because nobody wants to be called a custodian (too
close to janitor)

• “Data Governance Coordinator” is often a useful title but downplays the active
role that this person should have. “DG Lead” may be a better fit when this
person is really pushing the initiative forward

• “Executive Sponsor” is often important but can feel like a bull’s-eye too – it

might be even better to have an “Executive Committee,” which connotes a more
active presence by multiple senior stakeholders

• “Data Governance Council” is good for the ultimate steering authority

underneath executive leadership

• Other titles should be created for the roles as they are defined or coalesce

naturally – but in some organizations avoiding titles entirely is a better way to go

SAMPLE JOB DESCRIPTIONS

• Especially if sourcing candidates internally, people are unlikely to be doing

these roles already

• It is best to explain the activities and responsibilities as thoroughly as possible

while working with titles that can be interpreted in a variety of ways

• For example, a data steward position description could include:

• Lead efforts to define commonly used data with real-world language, help

Change Management

PRIORITIZATION AND PROJECTS

• We’ve talked about Data Governance being a change management function, but

we haven’t addressed how it fits alongside everything else

• Because Data Governance can align easily to existing change initiatives – and
because change initiatives tend to have material data impacts, this is important

• Most organizations treat existing project and program management/PMO

separate from Data Governance – this is logical, but often are missed
opportunities to coordinate between the two

• By itself, Data Governance needs to prioritize activities and conduct projects

• These can be managed independently, but there are risks anytime Data

Governance is treated as different from anything else

• Be proactive and reach out to other change groups – we may still keep DG as an
island, but trying to coordinate will likely lead to some goodwill, if  nothing else


--- PAGE BREAK ---

TIME-MANAGEMENT TECHNIQUES BUSY PEOPLE
USE, AND HOW TO KEEP THEM ON TRACK

• People love to wear their busyness like a badge of honor

• They tend to have more meetings the higher they move in the

organization

• There is a trend towards favoring instant messages and texts over

emails

• People will skip long emails and may not get back to them

• Norms vary greatly from organization to organization

• Visibility and reminders are effective in motivating people to meet

their obligations

• Be careful about calling people out and making them an example –
it can have a negative effect on everybody, especially longer-term

IDENTIFYING AND MAKING TRADEOFFS

• We cannot overstate the importance of being deliberate in prioritization and

making tradeoffs

• Being transparent and clearly communicating options and decisions is as

important as anything

• If those with actual authority fail to decide, we need to have a way of moving

forward

• Do not be afraid to suggest recommendations – this provides others an easy
out (to agree) on items that they do not care much about, but gives them the
option to exert more mental energy on those topics of particular interest

• Never trust people’s memory. This is when meeting minutes and

documentation become especially crucial


--- PAGE BREAK ---

FORCE-RANKING

• Force-ranking is when we have a clear, number-based ordering to a list (1, 2, 3, 4,

etc.)

• This is great for prioritization, especially when we have benefits and costs outlined

alongside any specific dependencies and constraints

• It is much more actionable than generic low-medium-high types of classification

• Under ideal circumstances, we would simply apply whatever resources to the

prioritized list in that order, and start checking things off

• In more realistic circumstances, we do have additional dependencies and constraints,

so we’ll often have to adjust the order of what we actually work on

• A force-ranked prioritization mechanism is easier to keep up to date with new items
and information, because the ranking aspect naturally drives more attention than
more vague mechanisms

HOW AGILE MAY (OR MAY NOT) FIT INTO THE
PICTURE FOR YOU

• Agile is a project management methodology embedded with techniques to

manage work while respecting tradeoffs and shifting priorities

• Agile principles have strong alignment to many of the things we’ve been

discussing

• In practice, however, it isn’t always easy to be Agile for Data Governance

workflows

• This is partially because Data Governance project teams tend to be part-

time, with an unpredictable quantity of work cycles available in each period

• It can also be difficult to implement Agile with folks unfamiliar with its

principles and norms

• The best general advice is to become familiar with Agile and find ways to

incorporate principles into your organization in a thoughtful way

HOW DATA GOVERNANCE IS MORE LIKE HR
THAN PMO
• Data Governance is definitely not a project with a defined middle or end

• It certainly has projects, but it is more than a collection of projects, which

is effectively what a PMO is

• Data Governance has a responsibility to interweave with existing
processes, project-based or not, if they influence data in any way

• Human Resources has a similar relationship with people, in that HR

provides functions and controls that support and guide humans as they
do other things

• For DG, this can manifest in many different structures or models (center-
of-excellence, shared service, cross-functional team, advisory board, etc.),
but the relationship inside the organization is relatively consistent

DEVELOPING PROJECT PLANS FOR DG

• Understanding that DG is a bit unique in where it sits in an organization,

conducting projects is still something that often comes up

• DG project teams are rarely all part of  the same team, and often cross over

business units

• Project participants rarely are dedicated to the project full-time, so this leads to

conflicting priorities and the potential for time allocation issues

• The DG organization should factor this into expectations for projects, and gain

clear and documented commitments for people’s time

• DG projects may have a second-citizen status, especially in the early days, so
planning contingencies for unexpected events may need more focus than for
“typical” projects

• While Agile can provide some benefits, it is better to fit within the organizational
norms for managing projects, as this will save political capital for issues that are
more connected to data (avoid rocking the boat unless necessary)

DG’S RELATIONSHIP WITH PROGRAM/PROJECT
MANAGEMENT AND CHANGE ADVISORY BOARDS

• Data Governance is not the only change agent in town, so let’s not reinvent the

wheel if  we don’t need to

• Most larger organizations have one or more coordinated efforts to manage change

• These change coordinators have been fighting some of  the same battles we need to

as Data Governance:

• Coordinating across business units

• Managing time commitments

•

Identifying dependencies and resolving conflicts

• They likely have been overcoming a series of  data challenges using uncoordinated
heroics on a project-by-project basis – or they may have caused data challenges for
others due to a lack of  awareness

• Either way, Data Governance is on the same team and can help these change

coordinators be more successful

COORDINATING WITH OTHER CHANGE
MANAGERS
• Do not, however, expect a warm response before earning it – these folks

can be protective of their turf

• Change managers also have to develop thick skin because they are often
viewed as a necessary evil, and get more than their share of complaints

• Data Governance, here as much as anywhere, needs a servant leadership

approach to provide value before asking for a lot of help

• If we can find ways to connect to other change initiatives and provide

service to them, we will be much more likely to be invited in to participate
more directly over time

• Some organizations have been successful with a combined DG/PMO

approach – this can work, but remember that they are not the same thing


--- PAGE BREAK ---

KNOWING WHEN TO IMPLEMENT REQUEST
MANAGEMENT AND TICKETING SYSTEMS

• Data Governance folks tend to be pretty well-organized and like to have things in their

place

• This leads to a tendency to overengineer processes and make things more laborious than

necessary

• A general sentiment of “earn the tools you buy” is a good approach, meaning that you

should feel a bit of a pain before making things more complex

• This is another area where, when ready to level up, solutions are already going to exist in

your organization

• This may also be a sign that your DG efforts have reached a point of needing to do more

formal coordination with other change management functions

• Try not to overdo it. The more effort required to facilitate Data Governance, the fewer

resources available to do the actual work of Data Governance

LEADING THE CHARGE WHEN YOU ARE NOT IN
CHARGE

• This is a consideration in a number of  contexts:

• When DG has no actual authority

• When we personally have little authority outside DG but are responsible for making DG

successful

•

In a culture of  drop-everything-for-today’s-emergency

• There is no doubt that this is a challenge under any circumstance

• The best approach is to focus on providing transparency of  information before trying to

change things

• This can be less directly confrontational, but still influential, as the problems become clear

•

If  we have suggested solutions at the ready, we’ll be able to plug in when we have the
opportunity

• Be careful to avoid being put in impossible situations while also not becoming someone

who only says no

HOW ALL SUCCESSFUL DATA GOVERNANCE
MUST OPERATE WITH LEVERAGE

• To have a meaningful impact in a large organization, Data Governance must

find a way to execute at scale without becoming a bottleneck

• This can be achieved through a combination of techniques:

• Education
• Delegating authority
• Decentralizing processes

• Clear and efficiently enforced guardrails
• Exception management

• Reviews and audits

• This is best when coordinating effectively with other change managers, but isn’t

absolutely required, especially early on


--- PAGE BREAK ---

DELEGATION AND ESCALATION TECHNIQUES

• We can push out DG-related decision-making as far as the edges of

our organization, but it will only work if the people making decisions
have the awareness and ability to make informed decisions

• Education and guardrails are important, but this may also be

uncomfortable for folks that operate normally under more direction

• Establishing a defined path and resources to help resolve questions
and provide guidance will help develop confidence and encourage
growth

• The ability to enforce authority will require the effective participation

of the rest of the DG hierarchy

• Do not forget to quantify the delegated activities and results – this

will help identify areas that need more attention

SCALING THE EARLY ORGANIZATIONAL
STRUCTURES

•

In the early days of  Data Governance, we may be starting with a handful or a
dozen regular participants that take on the brunt of  work

• Progress can be made, and big items like business glossaries of  important

definitions can provide a sense of  accomplishment

• By basing our internal success on how much is created, we set ourselves up for

failure when judged through the lenses of  results and impact

• As we create the structures of  Data Governance, consider how each person early

on will scale to a team of  people

• Allow for some flexibility in the mechanics of  making it happen, as being too

prescriptive may cause more friction than optimal

• Continue to find ways to measure effectively, and let that information drive

decisions, instead of  personal preferences or gut feelings

WHEN SENIOR FOLKS DROP THE BALL

• When we expand the Data Governance hierarchy and delegate responsibilities, we

will inevitably run into folks that don’t do what they’ve been asked

• This is a little easier to deal with for folks lower in the organizational hierarchy,

but what happens when the data owner or executive sponsor lets us down?

• First, summon a little courage because this is not going to be particularly

comfortable

• Next, talk to them and find out where their priorities are – this is best in a one-on-
one setting, as we do not want to call them out publicly until we have a clear
understanding of all sides

• We need to remind them of their importance to the success of Data Governance

• But we also need to remember that they are typically busy, important people who
may have had something legitimately more important going on – like testifying to
Congress or finding money for payroll

CHAMPIONING SUCCESSES TO DRIVE FURTHER
INVESTMENT

• This is an area we’ve touched on throughout the week, but it merits additional

emphasis

• Data Governance can be overlooked easily:

•

•

It is intangible

Its value proposition is as an intermediate function that helps other efforts be more successful

• Rarely does DG have conclusive and precise impacts

• Part of  the reason for quantification of  Data Governance efforts is to evaluate our own
performance, but part of  the reason is to lend credibility to our DG marketing efforts

• We have to become comfortable promoting the role of  DG, and soliciting others to

endorse DG’s impact

• Though it helps to tell others ourselves how much DG matters, our customers will be

more persuasive


UNDERSTANDING THE LANGUAGE OF EXECUTIVE
DECISION-MAKING

• Executives look at investment opportunities through three lenses:

• Costs

• Benefits
• Risks

• They look at calculations to determine Return on Investment (ROI), like Investment

Rate of Return (IRR) and Net Present Value (NPV)

• Especially given the nebulous and intermediary nature of Data Governance, we must
present a story that is even more clear and concise than other potential investments

• The audience is willing, if skeptical: Leaders understand that data is valuable, and it

makes intuitive sense that we should do something about it – but they typically do not
know themselves what form those investments should take


--- PAGE BREAK ---

DEVELOPING A BUSINESS CASE THAT BLOWS
THEM AWAY

• We have all the tools to make it happen, but most Data Governance folks

have little experience crafting extensive business plans or persuasive
presentations

• Many of us are a little too deeply involved in the details to know the key

points to articulate

• Just like when we find ourselves overwhelmed by technical complexity,

this is a situation where we should reach out for help

• Recall that marketing folks can be some of our best data people, and they

will also have familiarity with these kinds of situations

• This is also where relying on data can help us build our case, and a
prototype or proof of concept may also be a powerful addition

DELIVERING BIG ON PROMISES

• We might need to make some relatively big promises to get Data

Governance going, but the potential is even greater

• Perception of our effectiveness is awareness multiplied by actual

value

• Not to downplay the importance of quantification of our Data
Governance efforts, it is more important to actually create value

• Value comes from driving a material change in business outcomes,

measured by impacts to revenue, costs, and risk management

• This implies that Data Governance should not “stay in its lane” –
instead, find opportunities to help data work better anywhere in
your organization

• Many simple improvements can accomplish more in aggregate than
the big and complex initiatives, and the stories are easier to tell too!

Data Governance Careers

MOVING UPSTREAM FROM DG COORDINATION

• The beauty of Data Governance is that it can touch any area of an
organization and help influence how that area works with data

• There are a wide range of functions that come into play, and many

opportunities to forge interesting and beneficial networking relationships

• Moving into higher-level roles within the Data Governance organization is a
natural progression, especially when we have been involved from the start of
building it

• But often even more substantial are the tangential opportunities that come

from Data Governance work – moves into new departments or elevating our
responsibilities in our current teams

• The work of Data Governance also teaches us widely applicable skills that
come in handy, like building a business case or doing quantitative analysis


--- PAGE BREAK ---

THE MANY FACETS OF DATA GOVERNANCE
CAREERS

• Data Governance careers range from highly technical to senior leadership, and from

being a valued direct contributor to managing teams

• Data is also becoming important enough to most areas of the business that having a

strong foundation from the Data Governance perspective can set us apart

• These roles do not even need to be data-centric

• But going back to Data Governance proper, as DG scales it requires higher-level

personnel to guide it

• These folks can be similar to other change management leaders, who often occupy
positions that link the tactical execution of change initiatives to the strategic vision
of executive leadership

• And data is becoming an ever-bigger part of the strategic equation

THE INCREASINGLY STRATEGIC ROLE OF DATA

• Data used to be a competitive differentiator, where some organizations built

data-driven organizations that were especially responsive and nimble, and they’d
often crush their legacy competitors who had not paid as much attention to data

• But it took time, and that time has now passed

• Today, advanced data capabilities are table stakes, meaning that to have any

kind of long-term viability as a business, we must embrace data-driven
improvements

• Just as our organizations’ relationships with data has evolved, so too will Data

Governance need to evolve as well

• The Data Governance of 10 or 20 years ago will not be sufficient today, nor will
today’s Data Governance be able to accommodate the demands of tomorrow


--- PAGE BREAK ---

HOW SIMPLICITY IS INCREDIBLY COMPLEX

• Powerful and complex runs opposite to nimble and simple

• But we want our data to be all of the above

• This seemingly paradoxical state is actually possible, but requires

refinement and an ability to dial in granularity on demand

• Like so many things, our ability to communicate with one another

through data systems is what limits the capabilities

• The data is there, and the knowledge can usually be found, but

understanding depends on context – not just of metadata, but on
the ability of the recipient to interpret the information correctly

• If you do this long enough, everything looks like a data system,

and sharing of information is just another form of ETL

CHIEF LINGUISTICS, STARTING WITH THE CDO

• A Chief Data Officer (CDO) is a relatively new concept,

becoming somewhat common over the last decade

• The CDO, and companion Chief Analytics, Digital, etc., roles
are trying to elevate the strategic side of data capabilities to the
C-Level

• Though it sounds like it should be part of this group, the Chief
Information Officer (CIO) title was wasted on an attempt to
make the Chief Technology Officer be more business-focused
(it didn’t really work out)

• So now we’ve decided to try harder and basically do the same

thing again

• But now watch out for CDOs that have no actual authority (or
teams) – while somebody meant well, this is most assuredly not
a C-Level position


--- PAGE BREAK ---

UNDERSTANDING THE ROLE OF A CHIEF DATA
OFFICER

• A CDO is the ultimate sponsor of  data capabilities inside the organization – they are the

number one get-stuff-done-with-data advocate

• Data Governance will almost certainly roll up to a CDO, if  one exists

• The CDO should have a strong grasp of  the business, including deep perspectives on

operational and strategic considerations

• The CDO should also have a strong technology proficiency, as data needs sophisticated

systems to drive the business

• Data warehouses, advanced analytics, visualizations, etc. – these don’t just happen

successfully without a technology and business partnership, and the CDO is that connection
point

• There is much debate about where the CDO fits in an organization, but it depends – there is

no one-size-fits-all answer, but the debate can reveal much more than simply an
organizational structure
